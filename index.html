Getting started/ pre- class prep

The temperature of the water should be warm - you can use an electric kettle to bring water to a boil in advance of the class so that it is just warm by the time it is handed out to the class.

Troubleshooting

Plugging in the grove connector to add the external sensors to the Carrier Board only works in one direction. The white plastic connector plug has an orientation so remind students not to force the plug if it doesnt go in, and try lining up the grooves in the white plastic connector. One side of the connector wires plugs into the sensor, and the other side gets plugged into the carrier board!

Topics and keywords

  • Conduction

    : The movement of heat within a solid or between solids that are in contact. Heat naturally flows from a region of high temperature to one of lower temperature, until both regions have the same temperature.

  • Convection

    : The heating of a fluid (a liquid or a gas like air) causing the fluid to expand, becoming less dense, and rising while carrying the heat along with it.

  • Heat

    : Heat is the flow or transfer of thermal energy from one object to another.

  • Heat transfer

    : Heat is transferred from one object to another in three different ways by conduction, convection, and radiation.

  • Radiation

    : The transfer of heat from an object to its surroundings with visible and/or invisible electromagnetic waves. The hotter an object, the more it will radiate.

  • Temperature

    : The physical measurement of heat, or how hot or cold an object is.

  • Thermal Conductivity

    : A measure of the ability of a material to conduct heat.

  • Thermal Resistivity

    : The inverse of thermal conductivity. Materials with high resistivity are called insulators and have low thermal conductivity.

  • Thermometer

    : An instrument that measures temperature, often in Celsius or Fahrenheit.

  • Work

    : The transfer of energy from one form into another as the result of an external force acting on the object.

Learning outcomes

On completion of the experiment, students will be able to:

  • Measure the rate of cooling of water.
  • Compare the thermal conductivity of different materials.
  • Identify materials that are thermal conductors or thermal insulators, based on experimental observations.
  • Recognize the type of heat transfer that materials can reduce.
  • Investigate the effect of surrounding temperature on the rate of cooling.

Magic shows were common at fairs until the early 20th century. One of the most important modern entertainers was Jean Eugene Robert-Houdin, who later inspired Ehrich Weiss also known as Harry Houdini. Jean Eugene Robert-Houdin performed his art of magic in his Parisian magic theatre in 1845.

Background

Thermodynamics is the branch of physics which studies temperature and heat in relation to energy and work.

In 1871, James Clerk Maxwell described in the Theory of Heat, four important aspects that help us better understand what heat is:

  • It is something which may be transferred from one object to another.
  • It is a measurable quantity, and can be calculated mathematically.
  • It cannot be treated as a material substance, because it may be transformed into something else, e.g. steam engines which use heat to create mechanical movement.
  • Heat is one of many forms of energy.

In this experiment, we will use energy in the form of an electric kettle or gas from a stove to heat up water. Once the water is heated, and you leave it for a while, it will cool down - just like you may have experienced with a hot bowl of soup or a cup of tea. Eventually, if you wait long enough, it will cool down so you can drink it!

This cooling is called heat transfer, which is the movement of thermal energy from a hotter object to a cooler object. In the case of conduction, the transfer of thermal energy happens when two different objects are touching and have different temperatures. Heat spontaneously flows from high to low temperatures. Once the two objects reach the same temperature, the heat transfer stops. Thermal equilibrium has been reached!

Heat transfer by movement of fluid, such as a liquid or gas, is called convection. Heat transfer also happens by radiation, as all objects release thermal radiation. Thermal radiation produces an electromagnetic wave known as infrared radiation (IR). You can feel this radiation, for example when you are standing near a fireplace or stove even if you are not touching it.

The purpose of insulation is to lower the amount of heat transfer from a hot object to its surroundings (usually air) by wrapping or covering the object with a material. An insulator can either reflect the thermal radiation or decrease thermal conduction and convection. A good thermal insulator conducts little heat since it has low thermal conductivity. A good thermal conductor has high thermal conductivity but it is not a good insulator.

Temperature, in this experiment, is measured using the Arduino Science Carrier Board and an external thermometer. Temperature is measured in degrees Celsius (named after Anders Celsius, symbol C) or degrees Fahrenheit (named after Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit, symbol F). Water boils at 100 degrees Celsius (212 degrees Fahrenheit), and freezes at 0 degrees Celsius (32 degrees Fahrenheit)!

In the world many countries use the Celsius scale to measure temperature. Recently, the Fahrenheit scale was officially declared the temperature scale in the United States, Palau, the Federated States of Micronesia, the Marshall Islands, the Bahamas, the Cayman Islands and Liberia. There is one temperature value that is the same in both the Celsius and Fahrenheit scales try discovering this value yourself!

Collecting Data

  1. Open the Arduino Science Journal app.
  2. Start a new experiment.
  3. Select the sensor icon in the bottom tool drawer.
  4. Select Input 1 Temperature sensor in settings and choose the Celsius or Fahrenheit setting.
  5. Tap the record button when instructed by your teacher, so that everyone in the class starts recording their data at a similar time.
  6. Wait for 30 minutes as the heat from the jar is transfered.
  7. Tap the stop recording button when instructed by your teacher.
  8. Change the title of the recording to the material you used for insulation/conduction.

Results and Analysis

  • How did the temperature of the jar change over time? Is the graph a straight line or a curve?
  • Calculate the temperature change of the jar with each type of insulation material.
  • Which insulation material caused the temperature to cool down the slowest? The fastest? Why?
  • What kind of heat transfer is acting during the experiment?
  • How could you make the water cool down even faster?
  • How does the surrounding environment influence the transfer of heat from the jar?
  • What is the lowest temperature the water in the jar can reach? Why?
  • What are some everyday examples of thermal insulators?
  • What are some everyday examples of thermal conductors?

Future directions, next steps

  • Investigate the effect of surrounding temperature on the cooling of the jar. The jar can be placed in a refrigerator or a freezer, or outside on a hot day in a sheltered spot.
  • Investigate the influence of convection on the cooling process. You could remove the lid and place a fan close to the jar. The speed of the fan might affect the cooling due to convection.
  • The size of the jar or the amount of water might influence the cooling rate. The experiment can be repeated with the same amount of water in different size jars, or different volumes of water in the same size jar.
  • The effect of color of the jar/container on cooling by radiation can be investigated by using polystyrene containers painted with different colours instead of jars. Heating can be explored by placing different colored containers in the sun or under a hot lamp.
